Asics

The history of ASICS began on September 1, 1949[2] in post-war Japan. Kihachiro Onitsuka (鬼塚 喜八郎) thought about how he could help the Japanese youth in such a difficult time for the country[3] and decided to create a sports brand for everyone to inspire his fellow citizens through sports[3]. Thus, Onitsuka Tiger was born. In the early days of its existence, the company supplied goods only to the domestic market of Japan and China, and Nike founders Phil Knight and Bill Bowerman took advantage of this by buying sneakers and reselling them in the United States [4]. In 1977, after expansion, Onitsuka Tiger was renamed ASICS, an acronym for "Anima sana in corpore sano", which is Latin for "A healthy mind in a healthy body". A sports research institute was built in the city of Kobe to study and design running shoes.

Converse

In 1908, Marcus Mills Converse (born Marquis M. Converse), who had previously worked for the Boston Rubber Shoe Company, which specialized in the production of winter shoes, founded his own Converse Rubber Shoe Company in Malden, Massachusetts. At first, the main products were family shoes, for summer and winter. Two years after the founding of the company, more than 4 thousand pairs of shoes were produced every day. Sports shoes in the assortment of the company appear only after 1915. An important milestone in the history of the company is considered 1917, this year was the beginning of the release of Converse special shoes for basketball players - Converse All Star. Soon the famous basketball player Charles H. "Chuck" Taylor (Eng. Charles H. "Chuck" Taylor) from the team "Akron Firestones" begins to play exclusively in the "converse". In 1918, Taylor receives support from Converse and his first pair of All Star promotional shoes. Chuck Taylor officially joined Converse in 1921, and in 1923 the All Stars became his signature pro model after publishing a retrospective celebrating the 60th anniversary of basketball as a sport and teaching the first ball lessons at North Carolina State University. Chuck then went on a 35-day promotional tour to introduce basketball to America, earning him the nickname "basketball ambassador".

Adidas ClimaCool 1

In 2002, adidas released the Clima Cool 1, forever changing the idea of breathable footwear. They were produced at 360 degrees. what was previously possible impossible - ventilation occurs through the rear, toe, sides and even a hole in the sole. Although such a development was ahead of its time, ClimaCool 1 tried most of the time in the archive, only returning in 2016.At the heart of the design was the Clima Cool technology of the same name, a light and porous material capable of passing a large amount of air through itself. It was the material used to make the upper of the Clima Cool 1. Now its use includes t-shirts, trousers, sweaters and jackets for running and football, but in 2002 it made its debut through an unusual and advanced shoe silhouette. In many ways, this unusualness was the cause of the initial failure. Despite many experimental solutions on the threshold of the 21st century, including in the fashion industry, consumers were clearly not ready to change their usual suede and leather for something else. Radical changes were repulsive and took too long to be accepted. Such a fate befell both Nike's Foamposite and adidas' Clima Cool - not even a high-profile marketing campaign, accompanied by a release in honor of the Winter Olympics and a collaboration with Coca-Cola, helped.
